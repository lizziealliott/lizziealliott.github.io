IS US HEALTH INEQUALITY SHORTENING LIVES?

MOTIVATION
This regression outlines the inefficient nature of the US healthcare system. Despite spending over at least 2 percentage points of their GDP more than any other country, the US’ average life expectancy is below 80 years and is falling (Shmerling, 2022). As a potential explanation for the lack in outcome from expenditure, this project aims to present data surrounding inequalities in health between different groups of people in the USA.
This chart uses the World Bank Data python library to download the World Bank API - the data will refresh automatically when the code is run. I merged the 2 datasets via Pandas in colab. I couldn’t use a loop to download both datasets at once, so had to repeat the code for life expectancy and expenditure.
LOCATION - INSURANCE
The most notable way the US differs from other industrialized countries is the absence of universal health insurance coverage. This map aims to show the geographical inequality with regards to insurance across US counties. There seems to be a large disparity between the north and south. Texas sees uninsured rates of up to 37%; Massachusetts as low as 3%. However, there are some anomalies, like South Dakota.
I downloaded the ASPE CSV locally and used the geoJSON from GitHub to combine both datasets in Vega-Lite. Each county had a 2 letter code for its state in the same cell as the name. To clean the data, I extracted the characters from the string in excel.
RACE - INFANT MORTALITY
This chart aims to show how your race impacts your chance of survival before you are even born. Shockingly, by 2016, having a Black/African American mother would increase your infant mortality rate by up to over 5%, reaching the average of less economically developed ‘Upper Middle Income’ Countries.
This data was accessed via the CDC API and a loop over the Fred API. I converted both JSONs to csv, as it was easier to clean and merge the data. When I converted the Fred JSON, all the data was as a string in one cell, so I used excel to extract the characters from the string.
AGE AND GENDER - DISABILITY AND HEART DISEASE
This chart aims to explore the extent to which your gender and location impact the chance of you having a disability in the US. It is clear disability rates are far higher in women and some states even vary by over 10 percentage points. However, this could be due to lack of diagnoses in some men and states.
Originally, I had used the CDC API on disability rates across gender, age and state. But the JSON only included several states, so I struggled to find a y axis to compare genders simultaneously. I downloaded the new csv locally and cleaned the data in excel.
This chart shows how the chance of having heart disease differs by almost 30% depending on whether you are an 18-44 year-old women or over 65 man.
I really struggled to find data on different age brackets. Once I found this dataset, I downloaded it locally from CDC and used pandas to clean and convert it to a tidy, long-form format.
EDUCATION - SUICIDE
This chart aims to show the link between the chance of receiving at least a bachelor’s degree and chance of suicide in a given state. It’s clear that there’s a link between higher rates of education and lower suicide rates.
I used the US Census Bureau’s API for the data on population. The education data was collected by scraping Wikipedia. The rest of the data was downloaded locally. The datasets were merged all through Python Pandas. I had to drop District of Columbia, Puerto Rico and US as a whole. I also converted the string % values to numerical in excel, as the code wouldn’t work in python.
INCOME AND WEALTH - LIFE EXPECTANCY
These regressions show the link between the mean income and wealth/GDP of a given state on its average life expectancy. Interestingly, the mean income has a much stronger correlation with life expectancy than the GDP of the state. Perhaps this links to insurance and lack of national health service: it doesn’t matter if your state is wealthier if you cannot personally pay for your healthcare.
The data on nominal GDP per capita was scraped using beautiful soup in python from Wikipedia. The rest was downloaded locally from the World Population Review and merged in excel.
CONCLUSION
Overall, there is a clear link between race, age, income, gender and geographical location and numerous health factors in the USA. Perhaps, if these inequalities were improved, the excessive health expenditure may feed through onto improving the life expectancy of all American citizens.
